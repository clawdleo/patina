What Makes a Rare Marque?

Beyond Vintage. Beyond Collectible.

Most vintage mountain bikes are collectible ‚Äî well-made, period-correct, appreciating in value. Klein, Yeti, GT Zaskar, Specialized Stumpjumper: iconic, desirable, worth hunting. But "rare marque" status requires something more.

A Rare Marque is a manufacturer whose mountain bike production was so limited ‚Äî whether by choice, circumstance, or the sheer difficulty of the materials involved ‚Äî that surviving examples can be counted in the hundreds. Often in the dozens. Sometimes fewer.

These are the bikes that professional appraisers pause at. That serious collectors photograph from every angle before touching. That museum curators answer emails about at midnight.

The History: Italy's Carbon Pioneer

ALAN ‚Äî short for Alluminio Anodizzato ‚Äî was founded in Padova, Italy in 1972 by Faliero Masi's former framebuilder. The company pioneered a revolutionary construction method: carbon fiber main tubes bonded to precision-machined aluminum alloy lugs using aerospace epoxy. No welding. No brazing. Pure adhesion engineering.

By the mid-1980s, ALAN frames were racing the Tour de France. Their road bikes ‚Äî ridden by Hinault, Moser, and Saronni ‚Äî were the most technologically advanced frames in professional cycling. While other manufacturers were still welding steel, ALAN was bonding carbon.

Then came mountain bikes. As the MTB boom swept Europe in the late 1980s, ALAN applied their carbon-aluminum bonding technology to off-road geometry. The result was the Carbonio MTB ‚Äî arguably the first production carbon fiber mountain bike frame ever built. Not a prototype. Not a race-only special. A production bike, available in ALAN's catalog.

Production was tiny. ALAN's MTB operation was a side project compared to their road work, and the carbon-lug construction was expensive and labor-intensive. Estimates suggest fewer than 200 complete mountain bikes left their Padova workshop. Many were sold in Italy. A handful reached Germany, France, and Switzerland. Almost none went further.

How to Identify an ALAN Carbonio MTB

ALAN bikes are routinely misidentified ‚Äî and underpriced ‚Äî because sellers don't recognize what they have. A Carbonio MTB listed at ‚Ç¨1 "make offer" is not unusual. Here's how to authenticate one:

üîç Authentication Checklist

1
Head badge / Down tube decal

Look for "ALAN" in bold serif letters with the stylized "A" logo. Often gold or silver foil on a dark background. On Carbonio bikes, you may also see "Carbonio" script on the down tube.

2
The lug joints

The most unmistakable feature. Where most bikes have smooth, welded tube junctions, ALAN has visible machined aluminum lugs ‚Äî typically silver/natural anodized, distinctly industrial-looking. The carbon tubes terminate INTO these lugs.

3
Knock test

Gently tap the main tubes. Carbon fiber has a distinctive hollow, high-pitched tone ‚Äî clearly different from steel or aluminum tubes. The bottom bracket lug area will sound metallic.

4
Bottom bracket shell

Italian thread (36mm √ó 24tpi) in almost all ALAN frames. The BB shell is a machined aluminum casting, visually distinct from welded shells. Check for "ALAN" stamp or casting mark.

5
Serial number

Stamped on the underside of the BB shell. ALAN serial numbers typically start with a letter (production batch code) followed by 4‚Äì5 digits. No formal registry exists yet ‚Äî Pat Ina is building one.

6
Geometry confirmation

MTB geometry: shorter top tube (vs. ALAN road bikes), wider BB shell for triple chainring, cantilever brake posts front and rear, MTB-spec dropout spacing (135mm rear).

Common Fakes & Misidentifications

  • ALAN road bikes: Far more common than the MTB. Road frames have longer geometry, narrower rear spacing (126mm), often drop bar geometry. Worth ‚Ç¨300‚Äì800, not ‚Ç¨3,500.
  • Counterfeit decals: ALAN decals have been reproduced. Always verify construction ‚Äî fake decals on a steel frame are obvious when you check the lugs.
  • Other carbon-lug bikes: Vitus (French), TVT (French), and Pinarello made similar bonded-construction road bikes. These are NOT ALAN. Check the head badge.
  • Aluminum ALAN bikes: ALAN also made all-aluminum MTBs (no carbon tubes). Lower collectible value. Identify by uniform metallic tubing throughout, no carbon knock test tone.

Why Collectors Are Watching Now

The Italian carbon pioneer story is just beginning to be told in the MTB collector community. Road bike collectors have known about ALAN for years ‚Äî prices for ALAN road frames have risen steadily since 2018. The MTB variant is still largely undiscovered, particularly outside Italy.

The arbitrage window is open. A Carbonio MTB found on Kleinanzeigen.de for ‚Ç¨200 by an unknowing seller can realistically achieve ‚Ç¨2,000+ from a UK or Japanese collector who understands the provenance. That arbitrage gap won't last forever.

  • Carbon fiber MTBs from this era are extraordinarily rare ‚Äî most manufacturers didn't touch carbon until the mid-1990s
  • ALAN's Tour de France road heritage gives the brand legitimate provenance beyond just "vintage bike"
  • Japanese collector market pays strong premiums for Italian craftsmanship + innovation story
  • No major auction house has offered a Carbonio MTB yet ‚Äî that first sale will set a reference price
